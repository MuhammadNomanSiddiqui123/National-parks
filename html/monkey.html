Monkey Category

Macaques

Macaques are a diverse group of primates belonging to the genus Macaca, primarily found across Asia and North Africa. Known for their adaptability, these monkeys thrive in a variety of habitats, from forests to urban areas. They are highly social animals, living in troops that exhibit complex social structures and behaviors. Macaques are recognized for their intelligence and problem-solving abilities, often using tools and engaging in intricate social interactions. Some species, like the Japanese macaque, are famous for their unique behaviors, such as bathing in hot springs during winter. Their versatile diet includes fruits, seeds, and small animals, allowing them to thrive in different environments,Macaques are fascinating primates with a wide range of characteristics and behaviors. They typically have a robust body, long limbs, and a non-prehensile tail. Their fur color can vary significantly between species, ranging from light brown to gray or even black,Macaques are also notable for their intelligence. They can learn tasks quickly and are adept at problem-solving, which has made them a subject of scientific research. Aside from humans (genus Homo), the macaques are the most widespread primate genus, ranging from Japan to the Indian subcontinent, and in the case of the Barbary macaque (Macaca sylvanus), to North Africa and Southern Europe. Twenty-three macaque species are currently recognized. Macaques are robust primates whose arms and legs are about the same in length. The fur of these animals is typically varying shades.

Cebidae

Cebidae is a family of New World monkeys that includes species like capuchins and squirrel monkeys, primarily found in Central and South America. These primates are characterized by their high intelligence and agility, often displaying sophisticated social behaviors and problem-solving skills. Cebids typically have prehensile tails, which assist them in navigating their arboreal habitats. Their diets are diverse, consisting of fruits, insects, and small animals, reflecting their adaptability to various environments. Socially, they often live in cohesive groups, forming strong bonds through grooming and cooperative activities. Overall, Cebidae monkeys are notable for their complex behaviors and interactions, making them an important subject of study in primatology,Cebidae, the family of New World monkeys, includes notable species like capuchins and squirrel monkeys, which are renowned for their remarkable cognitive abilities and playful nature. Capuchins, for instance, are often recognized for their use of tools, such as using stones to crack open nuts. Females give birth to one or two young after a gestation period of between 130 and 170 days, depending on species. They are social animals, living in groups of between five and forty individuals, with the smaller species typically forming larger groups. They are generally diurnal in habit.Previously, New World monkeys were divided between Callitrichidae and this family. For a few recent years, marmosets, tamarins, and lion tamarins were placed as a subfamily (Callitrichinae) in Cebidae.

The night monkey

The night monkey, or owl monkey, is a unique primate belonging to the genus Aotus, known for being the only truly nocturnal monkey species. Native to Central and South America, these monkeys are easily recognizable by their large, expressive eyes, which are specially adapted for low-light environments. Night monkeys typically form monogamous pairs, with both parents actively participating in raising their offspring, highlighting their strong family bonds. Their diet mainly consists of fruits, leaves, and insects, and their nocturnal lifestyle allows them to avoid many predators active during the day. This distinctive behavior, along with their social structures and adaptations, makes night monkeys a fascinating subject of study in the field of primatology,Night monkeys, or owl monkeys, are intriguing primates recognized for their unique adaptations to a nocturnal lifestyle. Night monkeys are threatened by habitat loss, the pet trade, hunting for bushmeat, and by biomedical research. They constitute one of the few monkey species affected by the often deadly human malaria protozoan Plasmodium falciparum and are therefore used as experimental subjects in malaria research. The Peruvian night monkey is classified by the International Union for Conservation of Nature.
